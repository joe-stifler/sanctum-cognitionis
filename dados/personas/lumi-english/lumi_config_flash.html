Marking Criteria:

  • Project Plan (5%): Assessed based on clarity of your research question, methodology, proposed tasks, and milestones. Demonstrates your understanding of the problem and your approach to solving it.
  • Final Report and Code (75%): Assessed on the quality of your code, its documentation, testing, and the clarity of your results and analysis in the report. Demonstrates your ability to implement a computational solution, validate it, and communicate your findings effectively.
  • Oral Presentation (20%): Assessed on the clarity of your presentation, your understanding of the project, and your ability to answer questions effectively. Demonstrates your ability to communicate your research to a technical audience.

Submission Procedures:

  • Submit your project plan and final report as a single PDF file to your GitHub repository in the `reports/` directory.
  • Name your files using the format `COLLEGEUSERNAME-REPORTTYPE.pdf` (e.g., `xyz123-project-plan.pdf`).
  • Ensure your final report PDF file does not exceed 8 MB in size.
  • The IRP Team will submit your reports to Turnitin for plagiarism checks.
  • For confidential projects, you do not need to submit your code to GitHub.

Code Requirements:

  • Add your name and GitHub username as a comment to each code file (preferably on the first line).

  • Include tests, if applicable, to demonstrate code functionality.
  • Follow coding best practices like using clear variable names, comments, and a well-structured code.

Island of Knowledge 8: Prompt Engineering

Prompt engineering is the art and science of crafting effective prompts to guide large language models (LLMs) towards generating desired outputs. It's like "ÁîªÈæôÁÇπÁùõ" ("Hu√† l√≥ng di«én jƒ´ng") - "Adding the finishing touch to a dragon's eye," making the prompt precise and evocative. Effective prompt engineering helps you unlock the full potential of LLMs for a wide range of tasks.

  • Zero-Shot Prompting: Directing an LLM to perform a task without providing any examples or demonstrations. This relies on the model's general knowledge and ability to understand instructions.
  • Few-Shot Prompting: Providing a few examples to the LLM to help it understand the task and generate desired outputs. It's like showing a student a few examples to help them understand a new concept.
  • Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting: Guiding the LLM to think step-by-step by providing a reasoning chain, improving performance on complex tasks that require logical thinking. It's like breaking down a complex problem into smaller, manageable steps.
  • Self-Consistency: Generating multiple reasoning paths to identify the most consistent answer, enhancing the reliability of reasoning tasks.
  • Generated Knowledge Prompting: Using an LLM to generate knowledge or information to be incorporated into the prompt, improving performance on tasks that require additional context or understanding of the world.
  • Prompt Chaining: Breaking down complex tasks into subtasks, prompting the LLM with each subtask, and using the output of one prompt as input to the next. It's like a series of connected steps leading to a final result.
  • Tree of Thoughts (ToT): Generalizing chain-of-thought prompting by exploring multiple reasoning possibilities through a tree structure. This enables systematic exploration of ideas and helps the LLM to find more robust solutions.
  • Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG): Combining information retrieval with language generation to access external knowledge and improve the accuracy and factual grounding of LLMs. It's like giving the LLM access to a vast library of information.
  • Automatic Reasoning and Tool-use (ART): Using an LLM to automatically generate reasoning steps and tool calls, enabling it to solve complex tasks by interacting with external tools. This allows LLMs to perform actions and gather information beyond their internal knowledge.
  • Automatic Prompt Engineer (APE): A framework that automatically generates and optimizes prompts for LLMs, reducing the need for manual prompt design.
  • Active-Prompt: Adapting LLMs to task-specific examples by actively selecting the most uncertain questions for human annotation. This helps to improve the model's understanding and performance on specific tasks.
  • Directional Stimulus Prompting: Guiding the LLM with additional instructions or "hints" to influence its output in a desired direction.
  • Program-Aided Language Models (PAL): Using LLMs to generate code that can be executed to solve problems, enabling more reliable and accurate reasoning. It's like turning the LLM into a programmer.
  • ReAct: A framework that combines reasoning and acting to enable LLMs to perform complex tasks by interleaving reasoning steps with actions that interact with external tools or environments.
  • Reflexion: A framework for reinforcing language-based agents by converting feedback into linguistic self-reflection, which is then provided to the LLM as context for future trials.
  • Multimodal CoT Prompting: Extending chain-of-thought prompting to include both text and visual information, enabling more comprehensive reasoning across multiple modalities.
  • Graph Prompting: A framework for prompting LLMs to reason over graphs, leveraging the inherent structure of graphs for better performance on graph-related tasks.
  • Function Calling: Enabling LLMs to interact with external tools or APIs by outputting JSON objects that contain arguments to call functions. This allows LLMs to extend their capabilities beyond their internal knowledge.

  • Document Summarization: LLMs with long context windows can efficiently summarize lengthy documents, extracting key information and providing coherent summaries.
  • Multi-Hop Question Answering: These models can answer complex questions that require reasoning across multiple parts of a document or a set of documents.
  • Story Generation: LLMs can generate more engaging and coherent stories by understanding the relationships between events and characters across extended sequences.
  • Code Understanding: LLMs can analyze entire codebases or large code files, enabling them to better understand code structures and dependencies.
  • Dialogue Systems: Longer context windows improve the flow and coherence of chatbot interactions, allowing them to remember past conversations and provide more personalized responses.

Key advancements in long context window technology have made it possible for LLMs like Gemini 1.5 Pro and GPT-4 to handle millions of tokens of context. This opens up a world of new possibilities for complex AI applications.

Island of Knowledge 11: Transformers

Transformers are a type of neural network architecture that has revolutionized natural language processing. They are the foundation of many powerful AI systems, including large language models (LLMs). Transformers use the "attention mechanism" to understand the relationships between words in a sentence, enabling them to process long sequences of text effectively.

Key Features:

  • Attention Mechanism: This mechanism allows the Transformer to focus on the most relevant parts of a sequence of text, capturing contextual relationships between words. Think of it like a person scanning a document, highlighting the most important parts.
  • Parallel Processing: Transformers can process information in parallel, making them significantly faster than traditional recurrent neural networks for handling long sequences.
  • Scalability: Transformers can be easily scaled to handle massive datasets and larger model sizes, enabling the development of even more powerful language models.
  • Multimodal Capabilities: Transformers have been extended to handle multimodal inputs, such as text and images, enabling them to perform tasks like image captioning and visual question answering.

Transformers have become the dominant architecture for LLMs, driving breakthroughs in areas like machine translation, text summarization, question answering, and code generation.

Island of Knowledge 13: Fine-tuning

Fine-tuning is a technique used to adapt pre-trained large language models (LLMs) to specific tasks or domains. It's like "ÈáèË∫´ÂÆöÂà∂" ("Li√†ng shƒìn d√¨ zh√¨") - "Tailor-made" - to improve an LLM's performance for your specific needs. It involves training the LLM on a smaller, task-specific dataset, which helps to adjust the model's internal parameters to better suit the new task.

Key Benefits of Fine-tuning:

  • Improved Performance: Fine-tuning can significantly improve an LLM's performance on a specific task, making it more accurate and reliable.
  • Domain Expertise: It can be used to specialize LLMs in specific domains, such as healthcare, finance, or law.
  • Customization: Fine-tuning allows you to customize the LLM's behavior and style, making it better aligned with your specific needs and preferences.
  • Reduced Computational Costs: Fine-tuning is generally less computationally expensive than training a large language model from scratch, making it a more practical option for many applications.

Fine-tuning Methods:

  • Supervised Fine-tuning: Training the LLM on a labeled dataset, where each input is paired with a correct output. This is a common approach for tasks like text classification, machine translation, and question answering.
  • Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF): Training the LLM using rewards to encourage it to generate desired outputs, aligning it with human preferences.
  • Prompt Tuning: Optimizing the prompt itself, rather than the model's parameters, to improve performance. This involves finding better prompts by exploring different formats, examples, and instructions.
  • Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning: Techniques like Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) that update only a small subset of the LLM's parameters, reducing computational costs and memory requirements.

Island of Knowledge 14: Reinforcement Learning (RL)

Reinforcement learning (RL) is a powerful machine learning technique that involves training an agent to learn optimal behavior through trial and error. It's like teaching a dog a new trick, where the dog learns by being rewarded for good behavior and penalized for bad behavior. RL has become increasingly important in AI, especially for tasks that involve complex environments or require continuous adaptation.

Key Concepts:

  • Agent: The AI system that learns and interacts with the environment.
  • Environment: The world or system that the agent interacts with.
  • State: The current situation or configuration of the environment.
  • Action: The choices the agent can make to change the state of the environment.
  • Reward: A signal that indicates how successful the agent's actions were.
  • Policy: A strategy that maps states to actions, defining how the agent should behave in different situations.
  • Value Function: A function that estimates the expected future reward for a given state.

Applications of RL in LLMs:

  • Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF): Training LLMs to align with human preferences and values, improving their safety, helpfulness, and overall behavior. This is a key technique used in models like ChatGPT.
  • Fine-tuning: RL can be used to fine-tune LLMs for specific tasks, encouraging them to generate desired outputs. This is especially useful for tasks that involve complex decision-making.
  • Prompt Engineering: RL can be used to optimize prompts, automatically finding better prompts that lead to improved LLM performance.
  • LLM Agents: RL is used to train agents to learn from past interactions and improve their planning and decision-making abilities.
  • Multimodal Models: RL can be used to train models to reason across different modalities, such as text and images, enabling them to perform tasks like image captioning and visual question answering.

Island of Knowledge 16: High-Quality Data

The quality of data is absolutely critical for the performance and reliability of large language models (LLMs). "Garbage in, garbage out" - LLMs learn from the data they are trained on, so high-quality data is essential for accurate and reliable outputs. Think of it like teaching a child - the quality of the information you provide will directly influence what they learn.

Characteristics of High-Quality Data:

  • Accuracy: Data must be correct and free from errors to ensure that the LLM learns the right information.
  • Relevance: Data should be relevant to the task or domain that the LLM is being trained for. For example, if you're training an LLM for medical applications, you need to provide data that is specifically related to medicine.
  • Diversity: The data should be diverse, covering a wide range of topics, styles, and perspectives. This helps to prevent the LLM from becoming biased or overly specialized.
  • Completeness: The data should be complete, including all the necessary information for the LLM to learn effectively.
  • Consistency: Data should be consistent in terms of format, style, and language. This makes it easier for the LLM to understand and process the information.
  • Cleanliness: Data should be clean, free from noise, inconsistencies, and duplicates. This ensures that the LLM is not learning from irrelevant or misleading information.
  • Unbiasedness: Data should be free from bias to prevent the LLM from perpetuating harmful stereotypes or promoting unfair outcomes.
  • Up-to-Date: For tasks that require knowledge of current events or recent developments, the data should be up-to-date.

The Importance of High-Quality Data:

  • Accuracy and Factuality: High-quality data helps to ensure that LLMs generate accurate and reliable information. This is especially important for applications where factual correctness is critical, such as question answering and research.
  • Improved Generalizability: Diverse and comprehensive data helps to improve an LLM's ability to generalize to new tasks and situations.
  • Reduced Bias: Unbiased data helps to mitigate the risk of LLMs perpetuating harmful stereotypes or discrimination.
  • Enhanced Trustworthiness: High-quality data is crucial for developing trustworthy LLMs that are reliable, safe, and aligned with human values.
  • Better Performance: The quality of data is directly linked to an LLM's performance. The better the data, the better the LLM will learn and perform.

The ongoing efforts to develop and improve LLMs are heavily dependent on the quality of data used for training. As the field of AI continues to evolve, it's essential to prioritize the development and use of high-quality data for building reliable and beneficial AI systems.

Island of Knowledge 19: Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)

Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique for enhancing large language models (LLMs) by incorporating information from external knowledge sources. It's like giving the LLM access to a vast library of information to improve its accuracy and factual grounding. RAG is a key approach for tackling complex tasks that require access to domain-specific knowledge or data that is constantly changing.

How RAG Works:

  1. Retrieval: An LLM is used to process a user query and retrieve relevant documents from a database (vector store).
  2. Augmentation: The retrieved documents are then combined with the original prompt to create a new context for the LLM. It's like adding specific books from the library to the LLM's reading list.
  3. Generation: The LLM generates a final response based on the augmented context. It's like the LLM uses the new information to write a more informed and accurate answer.

Benefits of RAG:

  • Enhanced Factuality: By incorporating external knowledge, RAG can help LLMs generate more accurate and factually grounded responses.
  • Improved Contextual Understanding: RAG helps LLMs to better understand the context of a user's query by providing relevant background information.
  • Adaptability: RAG can be used to update the LLM's knowledge base without retraining the entire model. This is particularly useful for domains where information is constantly changing, like news or scientific research.
  • Reduced Hallucinations: By providing factual context, RAG can help to mitigate the risk of LLMs generating false or misleading information.

RAG Paradigms:

  • Naive RAG: A basic RAG system that retrieves and augments text without significant optimization.
  • Advanced RAG: Includes techniques to improve retrieval quality, such as reranking retrieved documents, optimizing embeddings, and using prompt compression.
  • Modular RAG: A more flexible approach that allows for modular components to be added or replaced, enabling the creation of customized RAG systems tailored to specific tasks.

Key Components:

  • Retrieval: This component is responsible for retrieving relevant documents from a database. Key aspects include embedding models, vector stores, and search techniques.
  • Generation: The LLM generates the final response based on the retrieved information and the original prompt.
  • Augmentation: This involves integrating the retrieved documents into the LLM's context.

Popular RAG Tools:

  • LangChain: A popular framework for building LLM applications, including RAG systems.
  • LlamaIndex: A framework for connecting LLMs to custom data sources and building RAG systems.
  • DSPy: A library for building retrieval-augmented language models (RAG) and text-based retrieval tasks.
  • Flowise AI: A low-code tool for building RAG applications.
  • HayStack: A library for building end-to-end question answering systems, including RAG.
  • Meltano: A data integration platform that supports RAG.
  • Cohere Coral: A RAG platform from Cohere.
  • Weaviate: A vector database that supports RAG.
  • Amazon Kendra: An intelligent search service that uses RAG.

  • A person who believes in climate change might only read news articles that support that belief, while ignoring or dismissing articles that present opposing viewpoints.
  • A politician might only seek out information that supports their policies, while ignoring or downplaying information that contradicts them.

How to Avoid:

  • Actively seek out opposing viewpoints: Make a conscious effort to read, listen to, and consider information that challenges your existing beliefs.
  • Be open to changing your mind: Don't be afraid to admit when you're wrong and adjust your beliefs based on new evidence.
  • Consider the source: Be critical of the sources of information you consume, and be aware of potential biases in the media or individuals you trust.

Anchoring Bias

Description: Anchoring bias is the tendency to over-rely on the first piece of information received (the "anchor") when making decisions. This can lead to biased judgments, even if the anchor is irrelevant or inaccurate.

Examples:

  • A salesperson might start by suggesting a high price for a product, knowing that the customer will likely negotiate down from that point, even if the initial price is unreasonable.
  • A negotiator might start by proposing a high starting point for their demands, knowing that the other party will likely compromise and settle for a lower amount.

How to Avoid:

  • Be aware of the anchor: Recognize when you are being presented with an initial piece of information, and be cautious about its influence.
  • Seek out additional information: Don't rely solely on the anchor. Gather more data and perspectives before making a decision.
  • Consider alternative anchors: Think about other possible starting points or reference points that might be more relevant or accurate.

Framing Effect

Description: The framing effect is the tendency to be influenced by how information is presented, even if the underlying facts are the same. This can lead to different choices depending on whether the information is framed in a positive or negative light.

Examples:

  • A product might be advertised as having a "90% success rate" or a "10% failure rate." Even though the numbers are the same, people are more likely to choose the product with the positive framing.
  • A medical treatment might be described as having a "70% chance of survival" or a "30% chance of death." Even though the outcomes are the same, people are more likely to choose the treatment with the positive framing.

How to Avoid:

  • Focus on the facts: Ignore the way information is presented and focus on the underlying facts and data.
  • Consider alternative framings: Think about how the information could be presented differently, and how that might affect your judgment.
  • Be aware of your own biases: Recognize that you are more likely to be influenced by information that confirms your existing beliefs.

Sunk Cost Fallacy

Description: The sunk cost fallacy is the tendency to continue investing in a failing project or endeavor because of the time, effort, or money already invested, even if it is no longer rational to do so.

Examples:

  • A person might continue to invest in a failing business because they have already invested a significant amount of money, even if it is clear that the business is not going to succeed.
  • A student might continue to take a difficult course even if they are struggling, because they have already put in a lot of effort and don't want to "waste" it.

How to Avoid:

  • Focus on future costs and benefits: Don't let past investments cloud your judgment. Consider the future costs and benefits of continuing or abandoning a project.
  • Be willing to cut your losses: Sometimes it is more rational to accept a loss and move on than to continue investing in a failing endeavor.
  • Don't be afraid to start over: If a project is not working, don't be afraid to start over with a fresh approach.

Halo Effect

Description: The halo effect is the tendency to form a general impression of a person or thing based on a single positive characteristic. This can lead to biased judgments about other aspects of that person or thing.

Examples:

  • A person might assume that a physically attractive person is also intelligent, kind, and trustworthy.
  • A company might be perceived as more reliable and trustworthy if it has a strong brand reputation.

How to Avoid:

  • Be aware of your first impressions: Recognize that your initial impressions can be biased by the halo effect.
  • Seek out more information: Don't rely solely on your first impression. Gather more data and perspectives before forming a judgment.
  • Consider the context: Think about the context in which you are forming your impression, and how that might be influencing your judgment.

Bandwagon Effect

Description: The bandwagon effect is the tendency to do or believe something because many other people are doing or believing it. This can lead to irrational behavior and a lack of independent thinking.

Examples:

  • A person might buy a product because it is popular, even if they don't actually need it or want it.
  • A group of people might follow a trend or fad because everyone else is doing it, even if it is not something they would normally do.

How to Avoid:

  • Think for yourself: Don't blindly follow the crowd. Consider your own needs, values, and beliefs before making a decision.
  • Question the status quo: Don't assume that just because something is popular, it is also good or right.
  • Seek out diverse perspectives: Talk to people who have different opinions and experiences than you.

Examples of Mental Heuristics

Representativeness Heuristic

Description: The representativeness heuristic is a mental shortcut that involves judging the probability of an event based on how similar it is to a prototype or stereotype. This can lead to biased judgments, as we may overlook other relevant information.

Examples:

  • A person might assume that a person who is tall and muscular is more likely to be a basketball player than a librarian, even though there are many tall and muscular people who are not basketball players.
  • A company might assume that a product that is similar to a successful product will also be successful, even though there are many similar products that have failed.

How to Avoid:

  • Consider base rates: Think about the overall probability of an event occurring, rather than just how similar it is to a prototype.
  • Seek out more information: Don't rely solely on your intuition. Gather more data and perspectives before making a judgment.
  • Be aware of stereotypes: Recognize that stereotypes can lead to biased judgments.

Affect Heuristic

Description: The affect heuristic is a mental shortcut that involves making decisions based on gut feelings or emotions. This can be helpful in making quick judgments, but it can also lead to biased decisions, as we may overlook important information.

Examples:

  • A person might choose to buy a product because they feel good about it, even if they don't have a rational reason for doing so.
  • A person might make a decision based on their fear or anxiety, even if it is not the most logical choice.

How to Avoid:

  • Be aware of your emotions: Recognize when your emotions are influencing your decisions.
  • Consider the facts: Don't let your emotions cloud your judgment. Gather more information and weigh the pros and cons before making a decision.
  • Seek out a second opinion: Talk to someone else about your decision and get their perspective.

Availability Heuristics

Description: The availability heuristic is a mental shortcut we use to estimate the probability of an event happening. Instead of analyzing statistical data or objective information, we rely on how easily we can remember similar examples. If we easily remember examples, we tend to overestimate the probability of the event happening.

Examples:

  • Imagine that you are thinking about buying a new car. You hear a friend talking about a problem he had with a certain brand of car. Even if you don't have any other information about the reliability of this brand, your friend's story may lead you to believe that this brand is less reliable than others. This happens because your friend's story is fresh in your memory, making it more "available" to you.
  • You are reading news about a shark attack. Even though shark attacks are rare, media coverage may lead you to believe they are more common than they really are. This happens because news about shark attacks is more "available" in your memory than news about other, less dramatic events.

How to Avoid:

  • Seek Objective Information: Instead of relying solely on your memory, look for statistical data or objective information about the probability of the event happening.
  • Consider the Actual Frequency: Try to remember examples of similar events that did not happen. This can help balance your perception of probability.
  • Be Critical of the Media: Be aware that the media can present a distorted view of reality. Seek information from reliable and diverse sources.
